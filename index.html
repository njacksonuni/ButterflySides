Butterfly Sides

A patchwork slidedeck of winged wanderers — stitched together one square at a time.

1

The Monarch Danaus plexippus

Orange-and-black wings like stained glass — a warning sign that says “I taste terrible.”

More about the Monarch

That bitterness is borrowed — caterpillars stockpile toxins from the milkweed they devour and keep them for life.

Fun stitch: Monarchs run a multi-generation relay marathon, traveling up to 3,000 miles to overwinter in Mexico — yet no single butterfly ever makes the full round trip. The great-grandchildren finish the journey their ancestors began.
2

The Blue Morpho Morpho peleides

An electric-blue shimmer that comes from light, not pigment — a trick called structural colour.

More about the Blue Morpho

There is almost no blue pigment here at all — microscopic ridges on each scale bend and bounce light so only blue escapes. Tilt the wing and the shimmer shifts like wet silk on a quilt.

Fun stitch: Snap the book shut and the morpho vanishes — the undersides of its wings are dull brown with owl-like eyespots, so a flying morpho seems to blink in and out of existence as it flaps.
3

The Black Swallowtail Papilio polyxenes

Midnight-velvet wings beaded with gold and blue, drinking from pink ironweed.

More about the Black Swallowtail

The bright spots and tail-like points aren’t just decoration — they mimic the toxic pipevine swallowtail to keep predators guessing.

Fun stitch: Its caterpillar has a hidden weapon: a forked orange gland called an osmeterium that pops out behind its head like a snake’s tongue, releasing a sharp citrusy stink to send ants and birds packing.
4

The Lime Swallowtail Papilio demoleus

A mosaic of cream and charcoal scales, dotted with red and blue, sipping from lantana.

More about the Lime Swallowtail

Also known as the chequered or citrus swallowtail, it is one of the most widespread butterflies in the world — a restless traveller found from the Middle East across Asia to Australia.

Fun stitch: Its caterpillars adore citrus trees, so orchard keepers know it well. Young caterpillars disguise themselves as glistening bird droppings — an unappetising costume that keeps hungry predators away.
5

The Common Blue Polyommatus icarus

A scrap of summer sky on a leaf — small, soft-blue wings rimmed with a fine white fringe.

More about the Common Blue

Only the males wear this bright blue; females are warm brown, often dusted with blue near the body. Despite its name, it is one of Europe’s most beloved little butterflies, fluttering low over grassland and meadows.

Fun stitch: Its caterpillars strike a sweet bargain with ants — they ooze a sugary fluid the ants drink, and in return the ants stand guard against wasps and other predators.
6

The Great Eggfly Hypolimnas bolina

Velvet-black wings stamped with white spots that glow electric blue-purple in the light.

More about the Great Eggfly

Also called the blue moon butterfly, the male flashes those shimmering haloes to defend his patch of territory. Females are master mimics, copying the look of poisonous species to borrow their protection.

Fun stitch: Great eggfly mothers are unusually devoted — a female will stand guard over her cluster of eggs for days, one of the few butterflies known to protect her young after laying them.
